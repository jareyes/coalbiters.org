Happy Birthday, Janet
98 and Counting

January 29 is Janet Halverson’s ninety-eighth birthday. Above is a picture of her (age 19) from the 1945 RISD yearbook, when she graduated with a degree in Advertising Design. We’d thought we’d throw a birthday party, but it’s cold and we just had an event and we’re not even certain she’s still alive. We think we know where she is, but we’ve reached out twice and not heard back. Still, we’ll celebrate her at least twice a week for the next three weeks as our current show is all about her work. Come celebrate with us before 6 pm on February 17 »

No Parking
Letter Study 01

If you missed Nina Stössinger speaking at ours on Thursday of this week, you missed a magical evening with a great talk, exceptional people, beautiful bites, and no complaints. Lucky for you, Nina bottled that magic in this collection of signs which discourage the parking of cars. A pleasant distraction from beyond our typographic bubble. Put your lips together and blow (a bubble) »

The Village by the River
In Full Color

Wang Hui-Ming was born in the People’s Republic of China in 1922, taught at Yale from 1951–61, then taught at UMass Amherst from 1964–89, illustrated a Gehenna Press book, made this print, and died in 2006. We count five colors printed via silkscreen + black printed via letterpress. This was probably a reject as it looks like a piece of furniture worked up in the press and started taking ink. Too bad. Otherwise a very nice thing. If you frame it right, you’ll never even see the mark. Maybe you could shave it away with care. Start shaving »

William Blake: Reader! Lover of books!
Declare Your Major

Do you even love books if you don’t have a William Blake broadside (printed via letterpress, obviously) that proclaims your love of said books? If you don’t have some such thing, we’ve got you covered. This broadside by Will Reuter of the Aliquando Press was printed in seventeen colors (we think) from Bembo and wood type types. Signed, numbered, and dated, it comes with a giant handwritten note from Reuter to his fellow printer Carol Blinn. Be a a good fellow »

Leonard Baskin: Sculpture & Drawings
The Bird is the Word

In 2021, after eighty-nine years, Mayflower Poultry in East Cambridge closed its doors and took down its famous sign which read Live Poultry, Fresh Killed. Maybe if they’d hung this Leonard Baskin poster in their window they could’ve lasted a little longer. (Or, maybe we could’ve walked down the street and bought some chicken there to help out.) Anyway, here’s a poster from LB’s 1969 show at the Borgenicht Gallery in New York City. Printed in two colors, what’s especially neat about this is that it was signed by the legendary pressman at Genenna Press, Harold P. McGrath. (Baskin’s signature is printed and not real.) A fine-looking bird. Let us give you the bird (in exchange for some money) »

Poppl-Exquisit
The Poster Specimen

We asked ChatGPT to “[p]lease write a 50-word description for a 2-sided poster made to promote the Poppl-Exquisit typeface.” It replied with Introducing Poppl-Exquisit Typeface: A harmonious blend of elegance and modernity. On one side, revel in its timeless serifs, capturing the essence of classical design. Flip it for a contemporary twist with sleek sans-serifs, embodying the spirit of today's aesthetics. Elevate your typography game with Poppl-Exquisit—where tradition meets innovation. That’s not quite accurate (at all), but it’s good enough for now. Sometimes it’s better not to say anything at. This one’s just a beauty. Be a beauty »

Once upon a time there was an Art Director
A Tragic Fable

We often note that one of the reasons we opened this place is because after we stepped down as president of the Society of Printers we missed doing things for people but not with people. This poster is a graphic depiction of that sentiment (with some good copy to help). Illustrated by Erwin Poell, there were other versions of this poster issued by Stempel. (We have another by Hermann Zapf which is too big for us to photograph.) A total charmer, this one would look good in your child’s room, giving them an early lesson in the fact that sometimes it’s good to keep quiet and let professionals do what they are trained to do. Do what you were trained to do. (Buy stuff.) »

Friedrich Poppl at the Klingspor Museum
All Around the Mulberry Bush

A while back we took in good stash of posters from the Klingspor Museum in Germany. As we flipped through them, one made us look twice. This one. What a beauty. The rubbish photograph we took doesn’t do it justice. Friedrich Poppl’s is a name we’ve come across more than once in our short life, and we could probably spot one or two of his faces, but this poster from the 1965 exhibition of his work has been a nice kick-in-the-pants to dig deeper. If you missed the show, put this on your wall and tell your friends you saw it. But first tell your friends you bought it »

An Alphabet by Hermann Zapf for Philip Hofer
Caps to Kill For

Imagine being in charge of a collection of books at Harvard and being able to ask Hermann Zapf to make an alphabet for you. Philip Hofer must’ve lived a pretty charmed life. Anyway, ol’ HZ scratched out this handsome alphabet for Phily in 1959 and surrounded it with a few thoughts about letters and reading and the like, each in a distinct hand. We’d have to do some research to confirm, but we bet this was printed at Meriden Gravure and was probably distributed to friends of Hofer. Be a friend »
